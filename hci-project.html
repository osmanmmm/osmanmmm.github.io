Project Description

CampusCare is a centralized platform that consolidates mental health resources for Carleton students. It simplifies access to wellness tools, reduces misinformation, and provides a peer support space. The platform integrates events, therapist information, and student groups to improve mental well-being at Carleton College.

Low-Fidelity Prototypes

How Our Low-Fidelity Prototype Meets User Needs

The low-fidelity prototype provided a foundational structure for CampusCare, ensuring a clear and accessible layout. It included key sections such as a centralized homepage, a wellness event calendar, and a peer support forum. The wireframes focused on defining user navigation and core functionality, allowing us to test and refine user flows before implementing a more detailed design in the mid-fidelity prototype.

Our Final Three Requirements

  1. Counseling Options & Shac Scheduling: Provides students with counseling services and step-by-step scheduling instructions. Click Shac Appointment to access this.
  2. Common Q&As & Blogs: Clarify misunderstandings through student bogs about mental health resources and address frequently asked questions.
  3. Resource Directory & Wellness Events: Centralize all mental health resources, including a clear calendar of upcoming events and workshops.

How Our Mid-Fidelity Prototype Addresses These Requirements

  • Counseling Options & Scheduling: We created a scheduling page with dropdown menus for appointment type, medical professional, and location. Users can see all available counseling services (SHAC, TimelyCare, peer counseling) and easily book a session.
  • Common Q&As & Misconceptions: Our prototype includes a FAQ section with items for each question. This helps dispel misinformation about mental health resources, transportation, and appointment costs.
  • Resource Directory & Wellness Events: We implemented a Resources page featuring multiple filter options (counseling, events, crisis help) and a separate calendar page listing upcoming wellness workshops and mindfulness sessions.

UX Research Process

We conducted semi-structured interviews with Carleton students and faculty to understand their current pain points and gaps in mental health resources. We then used affinity diagramming to group recurring themes, such as difficulty scheduling counseling, confusion around available services, and a lack of centralized resource listings.

Affinity diagramming: View Affinity Diagram

Analysis Process

Using axial coding, we analyzed these themes into our three core requirements: improving scheduling clarity, addressing FAQs & misconceptions, and centralizing resources. This process guided our mid-fidelity prototype design to directly target the most critical user needs.

High-Fidelity Prototype & UX Evaluation

Results of the UX Evaluation

What Worked Well

  • Users found it easy to schedule counseling appointments from the homepage.
  • The layout was more user-friendly and structured than existing Carleton websites.
  • Participants appreciated the wide range of resources and the direct scheduling feature.

What Needed Improvement

  • Some images were too large, making it hard to notice important links (e.g., transportation assistance).
  • Text size and color contrast needed adjustments for better accessibility.
  • Users suggested adding an immediate prompt to ask if they required transportation to Allina Health.
  • Some participants had difficulty finding peer therapy groups due to unclear navigation labels.

How the Prototype Evolved Through Evaluation

Reduced Visual Clutter

  • Resized large images on the homepage and resource pages.
  • Increased text size and contrast to enhance readability, especially for visually impaired users.

Added a Text Box for Appointment Notes

Users can now leave notes (e.g., “urgent appointment” or “follow-up request”) when scheduling counseling or medical visits.

Transportation Prompt

After booking an appointment at Allina Health, a confirmation screen now asks “Do you need a ride?” with a link to transportation resources.

Clearer Resource Navigation

  • Moved “Transportation Assistance” and “Peer Therapy Groups” to the top of the Resources page with clearer labels.
  • Ensured all major sub-pages include a “Back to Home” or “Back to Resources” button for easier navigation.

Overview of UX Evaluation Tasks

We tested three key user tasks during evaluation sessions:

  1. Finding different counseling options and scheduling an appointment.
  2. Locating transportation assistance.
  3. Finding a peer therapy group meeting on a specific day (e.g., Wednesday).

Overview of UX Evaluation Process

We utilized the following methods:

  • Think-Aloud & Co-Discovery
    Users verbalized their thought process while navigating the platform.
  • Focus Groups
    After each task, small groups provided feedback, discussed usability issues, and shared their experiences.
  • Informal UX Metrics
    • Time spent per page (in seconds)
    • Number of errors (e.g., mis-clicks, incorrect button presses)
    • Number of clicks required to reach a destination

Example User Feedback

  • “I love how easy it is to book an appointment from the homepage. But I wish there was a text box to explain why I’m booking.” – P1
  • “The images grab my attention, but they’re so big that I miss the Transportation Assistance link.” – P2

Informal UX Metrics Collected

  • Time on Page: Ranged from ~30s to ~60s per task.
  • Number of Errors: Most users made 1–2 mistakes (e.g., selecting "Direct SHAC Booking" instead of "Peer Therapy Group").
  • Clicks to Destination: Averaged 2–3 clicks; anything above 4 indicated difficulty with navigation.

Future Steps

  • Profile & Login Features
    Allow students to sign in using Carleton credentials to track appointments and visit history more seamlessly.
  • Enhanced Accessibility Features
    Implement stronger color contrast checks for colorblind users. Enable text scaling beyond browser defaults for improved readability.
  • Expanded Resource Categories
    Based on user feedback, introduce features like event reminders and integrated social support forums.
  • Secure Data Handling
    If personal health data is stored, implement security measures to ensure privacy and encryption.

Justification for Future Steps

  • “I’d love to see my past and upcoming appointments in one place, like a dashboard.” – P1
  • “If health information is stored, it needs to be encrypted or require secure login.” – P2
  • “I do everything on my phone, so a mobile-friendly version or an app would be really helpful.” – P3
